FTTP Electronic Trading Bot 📈

In March 2021, I was one of 60 college freshman selected to attend Jane Street's First-Year Trading & Technology Program (FTTP). Grouped into 20 teams of three, we competed in an Electronic Trading Challange (ETC), which had us program a market trader bot in Python that uses TCP connections and standard trading practices to operate in a simulated financial exchange. Our scripts used a known fair value of bonds, as well as discrepancies in the pricing of securities under the same underlying assets to generate profit. Some last-minute modifications to our algorithms allowed our team (Psyduck) to jump to 8th place in the final round, despite having spent most the competition in last due to some initial bugs!

Catch 21: Dice 🎲

This started out as my final project for CS 50! My partner and I implemented a web application to host a two-player dice game progammed with JavaScript. The game also features a username/password account system, a personalized game statistics page, and a global leaderboard for users to see how they stack up against each other, all implemented with SQL and Python. For the front-end, we used HTML5/CSS, as well as Bootstrap 4. We ended up deploying the website on Heroku, so anyone can make an account, play with friends, and appear on the leaderboard!

Keep on Trucking 🚚

In March 2020, I teamed up with four of my high school friends to compete in the MathWorks Math Modeling Challenge. Together, we researched the U.S. electric truck market, developed mathematical models (Python) to predict electric truck usage in the next two decades, and determined ideal economic/environmental zones for U.S.-based charging station infrastructure. We compiled our results into a final paper, which placed in the top 19% of 760 submissions.

Asteroid Ambush! 🚀

For my final project for AP Computer Science A, I programmed a 2D space fighter game that has users destroy asteroids and collect coins. The game features a timer, scoreboard, and life counter. To spice up gameplay, I added four different upgrade paths for the users to take, which can either grant lives, randomly destroy asteroids, or improve the ray-gun mechanism's rate and scope. The sprites were taken from open source locations on the internet, and the logic was implemented entirely in Java.
