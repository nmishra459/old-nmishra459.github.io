Research Projects


Deep Learning Algorithms for Following Zebrafish Growth

Zebrafish are great test subjects for medical research! But they all look similar, and they grow quickly, which makes it difficult for researchers to accurately identify them for trials. Working with my colleagues at Princeton University (Puchalla Lab, Dept. of Physics), I ran sections of the analysis pipeline for a deep learning recognition pathway (based off Google's Inception V3 model) that uses a temporal bootstrapping technique to identify individual zebrafish over several weeks (Python, Tensorflow, Keras). Our goal is to offer an alternative to more labor-intensive and invasive identification methods such as subdermal dye-injection and RFID tag implants. [publication in progress, supervised by Dr. Jason Puchalla]




Statistical Mechanics of Self-Gravitating Systems

In 2020, I worked as a research intern at the Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory (PPPL). As a part of PPPL's Advanced Computing Research Group (Theory Department), I focused on mapping out the statistical mechanics of self-gravitating systems as well as comparing them to those of plasma-based systems. As a high school intern, I developed an adaptive symplectic integrator (C++, Python) that can model the complex motion of dozens of closely distributed particles in space with reasonable accuracy. At the end of Summer 2020, I presented my work at PPPL's Summer Internship Poster Session. As an undergraduate researcher, I utilized these algorithms to study and record the underlying energy patterns and discrepancies in these systems. [supervised by Dr. Ammar Hakim]

A Microfluidic Flow System for Studying Single-Particle Kinetics

In the summer of 2019, I worked as a R&D Engineering Intern through Princeton University's Laboratory Learning Program (LLP). As a part of Puchalla Lab, I set up a multi-channel syringe pump system that facilitates single-particle kinetics in PDMS microchannels. To enable users to set, change, and read flow rates/directions, I programmed a user interface with LabVIEW, a graphical programming environment. I also worked on a set of MATLAB scripts that simulate the distribution of fluorescence bursts under various testing conditions. [supervised by Dr. Jason Puchalla]
